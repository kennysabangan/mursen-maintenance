# The Complete Guide to Winterizing Your Rental Property in Kentucky and Ohio

Winter in the Cincinnati metro area doesn't play around. When arctic air sweeps down from Canada and settles along the Ohio River Valley, temperatures routinely plunge below freezing, sometimes into the single digits. Ice storms sweep through Covington and Newport. Snow piles up in Florence and Fort Mitchell. And for rental property owners, that means one thing: risk.

A single frozen pipe can cause $5,000–$15,000 in damage. Heating failures can displace tenants and create liability nightmares. Ice dams tear off gutters and leak into apartments. Drafty windows drive up utility bills and tenant complaints.

But here's the good news: winter damage is almost entirely preventable. With a systematic approach and the right preparation, you can protect your property, keep your tenants safe and happy, and avoid those midnight emergency calls when temperatures drop.

This guide is built specifically for landlords with properties in Cincinnati, Covington, Newport, Florence, Fort Mitchell, Erlanger, Independence, and the surrounding metro area. We know this climate, and we know what it takes to winterize effectively.

## Understanding Our Regional Winter Climate

Before we dive into the checklist, let's understand what we're dealing with in the Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky region:

**Temperature Patterns:**
- Average winter lows: 20–25°F (-6 to -4°C)
- Typical cold snaps: 5–15 days per winter below 10°F (-12°C)
- Record lows: -20°F to -25°F in extreme events (1985, 1994, 2019 polar vortex)
- Frequent freeze-thaw cycles: Ice formation and melting repeats

**Winter Precipitation:**
- Annual snowfall: 15–25 inches in Cincinnati metro (more in Northern KY hills)
- Ice storms: 2–5 events per winter, sometimes coating everything in ½"+ of ice
- Nor'easters: 1–3 major storm systems bringing heavy snow and wind
- Wind chill: Along the river, west winds can make it feel 10–15°F colder

**Geographic Considerations:**
- Ohio River valley areas (Covington, Newport) often experience slightly milder temps but higher wind
- Higher elevations (parts of Fort Mitchell, Independence) see colder temperatures and more snow accumulation
- Urban heat island effect in downtown Cincinnati can reduce frost depth slightly
- Exposed properties on ridges or near open fields face greater wind exposure

This isn't Buffalo or Minneapolis, but our winters are serious enough that proper winterization isn't optional—it's essential risk management.

## The Complete Winterization Checklist

Use this comprehensive checklist to prepare each rental property before November. We've organized it by system and priority.

### Priority 1: Plumbing Protection (Do This First)

Frozen pipes are your #1 winter threat. Here's how to eliminate the risk.

**For Vacant Properties (Between Tenants or Seasonal Rentals):**

1. **Shut off and drain water lines** at the main valve
   - Open all faucets to drain remaining water
   - Flush toilets to empty tanks and bowls
   - Leave heat on minimum 55°F if property remains occupied
   - Leave cabinet doors open on exterior walls for pipes under sinks

2. **Insulate all exposed pipes**
   - Attic, crawl space, and basement pipes
   - Pipes along exterior walls
   - Outdoor faucet pipes (install Frost King covers)
   - Focus on first 3–5 feet from the point where pipe enters the conditioned space

3. **Install freeze sensors**
   - Place on vulnerable pipes (north-facing walls, attics, crawlspaces)
   - Connect to smart monitoring if you have it (Honeywell Lyric, Nest Protect)
   - Set alerts to trigger at 35°F or below

**For Occupied Properties:**

1. **Educate Your Tenants**
   - Provide a one-page winter readiness guide
   - Instruct them to:
     - Leave heat on when away (minimum 55°F)
     - Report heating issues immediately
     - Open cabinet doors on exterior walls during extreme cold
     - Let faucets drip during severe cold snaps
     - Report any frozen pipe signs (no water, whistling sounds, frost on pipes)

2. **Pre-Winter Plumbing Inspection**
   - Check for existing insulation gaps
   - Verify pipe locations and exposures
   - Install pipe insulation on accessible vulnerable lines
   - Consider adding heat tape to high-risk pipes (electric, thermostatically controlled)

### Priority 2: Heating System Service

A failed furnace in January is an emergency you don't want.

**Annual Service (Schedule by Early October):**

1. **Professional HVAC Inspection**
   - Heat exchanger inspection (cracked exchangers leak CO — deadly)
   - Burner cleaning and calibration
   - Blower motor check
   - Replace standard 1" filters (set tenant on quarterly replacement schedule)

2. **Check Safety Devices**
   - Test smoke detectors (replace batteries if needed)
   - Test carbon monoxide detectors (replace 5–7 years)
   - Install additional CO detectors if none on each level

3. **System Verification**
   - Confirm thermostat accuracy
   - Check heating vent output temperatures
   - Inspect gas lines, flues, and exhaust systems
   - Verify condensate drain is clear (for high-efficiency furnaces)

4. **Documentation**
   - Get a written inspection report
   - Keep records for each property
   - Provide copy to tenants (helps with accountability)

**Tenant Education:**
- Show them where the emergency shut-off is
- Explain not to block vents or registers
- Set clear response protocol for heating failures (24/7 hotline)

### Priority 3: Exterior Preparation

Don't forget what's outside. Your roof, foundation, and landscaping are all vulnerable.

**Roof & Gutters:**

1. **Fall Gutter Cleaning** (Sept–Oct)
   - Remove all leaves and debris
   - Check for proper slope and secure attachment
   - Inspect for rust, cracks, separation
   - Ensure downspouts extend 6+ feet from foundation

2. **Roof Inspection**
   - Check for missing, cracked, or curling shingles
   - Inspect flashing around chimneys, vents, and valleys
   - Look for nail pops or hail damage
   - Trim overhanging branches (especially those that could fall on roof during ice storms)

3. **Ice Dam Prevention**
   - Ensure attic is properly ventilated
   - Verify insulation meets code (R-38 or higher in attic)
   - Consider heated gutter cables in problem areas
   - Seal attic air leaks that let warm air melt snow on roof

**Foundation & Drainage:**

1. **Grading and Downspouts**
   - Soil should slope away from foundation (6" drop over 10 feet)
   - Extend downspouts with buried PVC or flexible pipe if needed
   - Install French drains if basement has water issues

2. **Foundation Cracks**
   - Seal foundation cracks with hydraulic cement
   - Repair major cracks professionally before winter
   - Check window wells for proper drainage

3. **Sump Pump**
   - Test operation before freeze-up
   - Install backup battery if not present
   - Insulate discharge line to prevent freezing

**Doors, Windows, and Openings:**

1. **Weatherstripping and Caulking**
   - Replace worn weatherstripping on doors
   - Caulk gaps around windows, baseboards, and penetrations
   - Install door sweeps on exterior doors
   - Check attic and crawlspace vents for proper closure

2. **Window Insulation**
   - Consider window insulation kits (plastic shrink film) for single-pane windows
   - Check that storm windows are in good condition
   - Ensure all windows lock properly to create tighter seal

### Priority 4: Interior Comfort & Safety

Inside the unit matters for tenant satisfaction and safety.

**Safety Equipment:**

1. **Detectors**
   - Working smoke detector on each level and in each bedroom
   - Working CO detector on each level (within 10' of bedrooms)
   - Replace batteries annually (best done in fall)
   - Units 10+ years old should be replaced

2. **Fireplaces and Wood Stoves**
   - Annual chimney sweep and inspection (required if used)
   - Check for cracks, damage, or Creosote buildup
   - Provide tenants with safe operating instructions
   - Remove stored ashes completely before winter

**Tenant Comfort:**

1. **Ceiling Fans**
   - Reverse direction to clockwise in fall
   - Run on low to push warm air down from ceiling

2. **HVAC System Care**
   - Replace filters quarterly (provide supply to tenant)
   - Keep registers and returns unblocked by furniture or rugs
   - Schedule any needed repairs before peak winter season

3. **Emergency Preparedness**
   - Provide tenants with emergency contact information
   - Include instructions for reporting after-hours issues
   - Consider providing basic emergency kit in common areas (ice melt, sand, flashlights)

### Priority 5: Landlord's Emergency Kit

Be prepared yourself so you're not scrambling during a crisis.

**For Each Property:**

- [ ] Copy of all utility shut-off locations (gas, water, electric)
- [ ] Tenant emergency contact info (current phone numbers)
- [ ] 24/7 vendor contact list (plumber, HVAC, roofer, electrician)
- [ ] Spare keys and code access info
- [ ] Property insurance policy details and agent contact
- [ ] Tenant lease start/end dates and move-out procedures

**Vendor Relationships:**
- Pre-qualify emergency vendors who service Covington/Cincinnati area
- Get after-hours rate agreements in writing
- Keep on file: license numbers, insurance certificates
- Test their response time before winter

## Local Considerations for Cincinnati & Northern Kentucky

Let's address specific issues we see in our markets:

**Older Housing Stock (Covington, Newport, Cincinnati):**

- Brick Victorians and shotgun houses often have gaps in exterior walls
- Knob-and-tube wiring (if present) presents fire risks with space heaters
- Uninsulated walls and attics are common
- Original single-pane windows create drafts

*Action:* Prioritize insulation and air sealing. Consider window insulation kits as low-cost improvement. Upgrade electrical panels to handle modern heating loads.

**Newer Suburban Housing (Florence, Fort Mitchell, Erlanger, Independence):**

- Generally better insulated but still need pipe protection
- HVAC systems may be newer but still need annual service
- Foundation issues can arise from poor drainage

*Action:* Focus on HVAC service and drainage. Check builder-grade pipe insulation—it may be inadequate.

**Multi-Unit Properties:**

- Shared walls can create unique cold spots
- Common areas often get neglected
- Tenant turnover may disrupt seasonal prep timing

*Action:* Create a property-wide winterization schedule. Use check-in checklists for tenant move-ins/outs. Keep common areas heated and monitored.

**Properties near the Ohio River:**

- Increased wind exposure
- Slightly warmer temps but more moisture
- Flooding risk in extreme events

*Action:* Focus on wind barriers (landscaping, fencing). Ensure sump pumps are in good working order. Elevate utilities in basements if prone to flooding.

## DIY vs Professional: What to Outsource

You can handle some winterization yourself. Here's what we recommend assigning to pros:

| Task | DIY Friendly? | Why/Why Not |
|------|---------------|------------|
| Gutter cleaning | Maybe | Risk of falls on ladders; better to hire insured pro for 2+ story homes |
| HVAC service | No | Requires certification; void warranties if DIY |
| Roof inspection | No | Safety risk; trained eyes spot problems you miss |
| Major pipe insulation | Maybe | Access issues in tight crawlspaces/attics; pro can do faster and safer |
| Chimney sweep | No | Fire risk; requires tools and experience; mess |
| Major air sealing | Maybe | Can be DIY but pros use blower door test for thoroughness |
| Sump pump repair/replace | Maybe | Mechanical skill needed; but many can handle |
| Foundation crack repair | Maybe | Small cracks DIY; larger issues need structural engineer |

**Our Recommendation:** Have a professional winterization inspection done annually ($150–$300) to identify issues, then DIY what you're comfortable with and hire out the rest. This gives you expert eyes without committing to full-service costs.
