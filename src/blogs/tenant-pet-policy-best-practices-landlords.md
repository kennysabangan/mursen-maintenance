# Tenant & Pet Policy: Best Practices for Landlords

Your lease is more than a formality — it's your first line of defense against property damage, lost rent, and legal headaches. A clear, comprehensive tenant and pet policy sets expectations and gives you enforceable standards.

Here's what every landlord should include.

## Tenant Policy Essentials

### 1. Rent Payment Terms
- Due date and grace period
- Late fees (amount and when they apply)
- Accepted payment methods
- NSF check policy

### 2. Occupancy Limits
Specify maximum number of occupants. This prevents unauthorized tenants and overcrowding.

### 3. Maintenance Responsibilities
- What tenants must report promptly (leaks, mold, pest issues)
- What they're responsible for (filter changes, light bulbs, yard care)
- Prohibited alterations (painting, fixtures, smart locks without permission)

### 4. House Rules
- Quiet hours
- Trash and recycling schedule
- Parking rules
- Guest policy (how long guests may stay)
- Smoking policy (specify if smoke-free, including outside areas like balconies)

### 5. Entry Notification
When and how you'll enter the property (generally 24–48 hour notice except emergencies).

### 6. Move-Out Condition
Expectations for cleaning, repair costs for damage beyond normal wear and tear, and security deposit return timeline.

## Pet Policy: Finding the Balance

Pets are a divisive topic. Some landlords blanket ban them; others welcome them with strict terms. The best approach is a clear, fair pet policy that protects your property while accommodating responsible pet owners.

### Pet Addendum Components

- **Allowed Species:** Typically dogs and cats. Exclude reptiles, amphibians, livestock, exotic animals.
- **Breed Restrictions:** Many insurers restrict certain breeds. List prohibited breeds according to your insurer.
- **Size Limits:** Some landlords limit weight (e.g., under 50 lbs).
- **Pet Deposit & Pet Rent:** 
  - Deposit: refundable (often $200–$500)
  - Pet rent: monthly fee ($25–$50 per pet)
- **Number Limit:** Usually 1–2 pets per unit.
- **Vaccination Records:** Require current rabies, DHPP, etc. for dogs; FVRCP for cats.
- **Spay/Neuter:** May require proof unless medically exempt.
- **Pet Liability Insurance:** Some landlords require tenants to carry renters insurance with pet liability coverage.
- **Damage Costs:** Tenant responsible for any damage beyond normal wear, including stains, scratches, odors.
- **Removal:** Right to require removal of pets that violate policy or cause disturbance.

### Service Animals & Emotional Support Animals

The Fair Housing Act requires reasonable accommodation for service animals and ESAs, even in “no-pet” properties. You may request:

- Documentation from a medical professional (not from an online registry)
- Assurance that the animal is house-trained
- That the animal does not pose a direct threat

But you **cannot** charge pet deposit or pet rent for legitimate service animals/ESAs.

## Enforcing Your Policy

Consistency is key:

- Apply rules equally to all tenants
- Document violations in writing
- Provide notice and opportunity to cure before eviction (as required by law)
- Keep records of all communications

## Maintenance Implications

Pets cause wear. Consider:

- Adding carpet cleaning to regular maintenance schedule
- Inspecting yards for holes, digging
- Checking for flea infestations
- Replacing HVAC filters more frequently (pet dander)

## Sample Lease Clauses

We recommend having an attorney review your lease. But here are guidelines for clear language:

"Tenant shall keep the Premises free of pets unless written consent from Landlord is obtained via separate Pet Addendum. Unauthorized pets constitute a breach of lease and may result in removal of the pet and/or termination of tenancy."
